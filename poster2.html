Background & Purpose

Created in 1936, this eugenics poster framed alcoholism as a hereditary danger to Germany. Claudia Koonz shows that the regime regularly transformed moral judgments into biological warnings, claiming personal behaviors threatened the ‚Äúracial health‚Äù of the nation. By portraying decline across several generations, the poster reframes social issues as genetic defects rather than medical or economic problems.

Where It Was Displayed

  • Health exhibitions and racial hygiene lectures
  • Public clinics and hospitals
  • School biology lessons
  • Pamphlets from the Office of Racial Policy

The USHMM notes these posters were placed in settings where people trusted medical authority, making propaganda appear scientific and credible.

Symbolism & Emotional Strategy

Nazi propagandists used a generational family tree to imply an unavoidable decline. Jeffrey Herf explains that diagrams like this gave propaganda an aura of scientific certainty, turning fear into support for state intervention. By ‚Äúpredicting‚Äù suffering in future children, the poster made sterilization seem protective rather than violent.

Historical Importance

Doris Bergen emphasizes that public acceptance of eugenics was essential for enforcing the 1933 Sterilization Law. Posters like this normalized the idea that certain families were burdens on society. Richard Evans notes that this rhetoric directly shaped the later T4 killing program. The poster is therefore an early step in legitimizing biological inequality and state control over reproduction.
