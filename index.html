Introduction

This project examines how Nazi Germany used mass media to promote its ideology of racial superiority and to make extremist policies appear normal, scientific, and necessary. Posters, newspapers, radio broadcasts, and public speeches did not simply inform citizens; they carefully shaped what people believed about race, community, and loyalty. By studying specific propaganda posters, we can see how visual design and language worked together to legitimize discrimination, sterilization, and eventually mass murder.

The Reich Ministry of Public Enlightenment and Propaganda, led by Joseph Goebbels, coordinated nearly every aspect of communication in the Third Reich. The ministry controlled newspapers, film, radio, theater, literature, and visual art. By limiting access to alternative viewpoints, it created an information environment where Nazi ideology appeared to be common sense.
